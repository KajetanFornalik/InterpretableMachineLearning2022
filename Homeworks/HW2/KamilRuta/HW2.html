California Housing price regression and SHAP¶

The report describes XGBoost and Multi Layer Perceptron learning results on California Housing dataset and decomposition of trained models using SHAP.

XGBoost model was trained with 1000 estimators, max depth equal to 5 and default settings from sklearn. On a validation set it got the following statistics: MSE=0.20, R^2=0.84. Below there are validation results:

Below there is a decomposition of selected predictions using SHAP. For the lowest price the most important features were latitude and longitude. Latitude had a large impact on decreasing house value and longitude incresed it, but overall the location had a negative impact on the value comparing to the mean. Average number of bedrooms, average number of rooms and medium income had lower impact on prediction results and contribution of other features was very low.

Decomposition for the block with medium prices shows similar results - latitude and longitude were the most important, income affected the price to a lesser extent and other features were much less influential.

In the last case longitude does not play such a big role, but latitude is still the most important feature. Importance of medium income is greater in this case and together with latitude these two features increase predicted price for about 2.4 * $100 000.

Comparing the medium and the most expensive block of houses we see how SHAP produces local interpretations of feature importance. For the medium price houses the prediction depens mainly on the location according to SHAP results. Looking at the case of the most expensive block, we see that medium income can be more important than one of the coordinates features.

Looking at the three diagrams we can also see that the medium income can have positive and negative effect on predicted prices. It decreased the price for cheapest houses and incresed it for the most expensice block. My hypothesis is that people with higher income have better home furnishings and because of that their houses are more expensive. There is no such a variable in the data, but medium income can be a proxy to it.

Below there is a SHAP summary for sample 100 observations. The plot shows a relationship between medium income and its importance on predicting prices - the greater the income, the more important it is in inreasing the price. There is a similar relationship for average rooms feature, but to much lesser extent. We can see that coordinates are the most important factor in decreasing the prices in general, but they are not the only important features incresing it.

from matplotlib import pyplot
import shap
from xgboost import XGBRegressor

from sklearn import metrics
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.datasets import fetch_california_housing
from sklearn.preprocessing import MinMaxScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor

def evaluate(testY, predY):
    pyplot.plot(testY, testY, 'r')
    pyplot.scatter(testY, predY)
    pyplot.title('Median House Value');
    pyplot.xlabel('Actual')
    pyplot.ylabel('Predicted')
    pyplot.show()

    print()
    print("MSE:", metrics.mean_squared_error(testY, predY))
    print("MAE:", metrics.mean_absolute_error(testY, predY))
    print("R2:", metrics.r2_score(testY, predY))

model_XGB = XGBRegressor(objective='reg:squarederror', n_estimators=1000, max_depth=5)
